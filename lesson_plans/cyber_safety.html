Safety & Privacy on the Web ~ Introduction for Youth

Made by Dumitru Gherman and Emma Irwin

The Web we know and love is not scary. The Web is a magical collaborative space where the possibilities for invention are endless; where problems are solved, and really, really human-experiences and kindness are magnified to inspire millions.


The Web can also be a scary, damaging place for kids and youth living online, and for the adults trying to keep them safe. This Hacktivity Kit will provide a lesson plan to help participants learn best practices through remix.


Steps for the Activity

  1. Discussion

    • Introductions: In a circle, ask everyone to quickly provide their name, and something they do to keep themselves or their family safe online.
    • Icebreaker: As a Spectogram (smaller group), or as a set of 5 teams (larger group) respond to the discussion statements (listed to the right of this page) which introduce essential themes (or add your own).

  2. Protect Your Privacy

    Ask participants to find five unsafe practices, describe their consequences, and how they feel in general about protecting personal information by remixing this video with Popcorn Maker.

    Introduce Popcorn Maker
    Suggested themes:
    • Don't leave passwords in open spaces.
    • Protect your personal information.
    • Don't leave your computer unlocked.
    • Secure ALL devices with unique and secure passwords.
    • Don't click on emails, or links from people you don't know, or that solicit personal information.

  3. Cybersafety

    Using X-Ray Goggles, hack Thomas Tabby's Facebook profile to make it safe using these best practices.

    Introduce X-Ray Goggles
    Suggested themes:
    • Don't share any photo publicly, you wouldn't share with your grandma.
    • Never disclose your whereabouts publicly, or disclose you are alone.
    • Respect the privacy of others, don't post or tag photos of people without their permission.
    • Do not meet, or give out your full name, address, or phone number to anyone online that you don't trust or know in person.
    • Never accept friend requests, or interact with strangers online. Understand that how people portray themselves might not necessarily be true.
    • Do be positive, enjoy sharing and making things made possible by the web.

  4. Next Steps

  5. Circle-up and ask each person to share how Webmaking can help teach Privacy and Safety to adults and youth.

Materials

  • Pens
  • Post-its
  • Computer(s)
  • Web Access

Discussion Statements

    • Only adults are concerned with online safety.
    • Activity online is mostly negative to my future.
    • Parental controls remove most risk for youth online.
    • Kids shouldn't have access to social media until they are at least 16 years of age, and aware of risks.
    • Kids should be allowed to access the web at school.
    • Implementing digital and web literacy practices into our daily routines can help keep everyone safter.
